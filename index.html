Clinical Development Pipeline

Pre-clinical → Approval

In India, drug development is regulated by the Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation (CDSCO) under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940. New drugs require permission before initiating clinical trials, which is granted through the IND (Investigational New Drug) process.

1

Pre-Clinical Studies

Conducted in vitro (cell lines) and in vivo (animal models) to assess safety, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, and toxicity of the drug candidate. Includes ADME studies, acute/subacute/chronic toxicity, mutagenicity, and teratogenicity tests.

In vitro studies Animal models Toxicology (GLP) ADME profiling Duration: 1–3 years
2

IND Filing with CDSCO

Submit an Investigational New Drug application to CDSCO (Form CT-04 under New Drugs and Clinical Trials Rules 2019). Includes the dossier with pre-clinical data, proposed protocol, Investigator's Brochure, and Ethics Committee approval.

Form CT-04 CDSCO review IEC approval Informed consent Timeline: 30–90 days
3

Phase I Clinical Trial — Safety

First-in-human studies conducted in 20–100 healthy volunteers (or patients with severe disease). Objective: determine safe dosage range, pharmacokinetics, and common side effects. Conducted at Phase I units approved by CDSCO.

20–100 subjects Dose escalation PK/PD studies Duration: 1–2 years
4

Phase II Clinical Trial — Efficacy

Conducted in 100–500 patients with the target condition. Evaluates therapeutic efficacy, optimal dose, and short-term safety. Randomised, controlled design. Results inform Phase III design.

100–500 patients RCT design Dose optimisation Duration: 2–3 years
5

Phase III Clinical Trial — Confirmatory

Large-scale, multicentre trials in 1,000–5,000+ patients. Confirms efficacy, monitors adverse reactions in a larger population, and compares to standard of care. Forms the basis for New Drug Application (NDA) submission to CDSCO.

1,000–5,000+ patients Multi-centre Double-blind RCT Duration: 3–5 years
6

NDA Submission & CDSCO Approval

Submit New Drug Application with complete clinical, pre-clinical, CMC, and pharmacovigilance data to CDSCO. Subject Expert Committee (SEC) reviews dossier. Upon approval, obtain market authorisation to manufacture and sell.

NDA dossier (CTD format) SEC review Form 45 licence Post-marketing surveillance

Licensing

Mfg · Sale · Import · Export

In India, drug licensing is governed by the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940 and Rules 1945. All licences are issued by State Licensing Authorities (SLA) or Central Licensing Authority (CDSCO) depending on the category of drugs.

🏭

Manufacturing Licence

Required to manufacture drugs for sale, distribution, or export. Issued as Form 25 (non-sterile) or Form 28 (sterile/biological).

  • Issued by State Licensing Authority
  • GMP compliance mandatory (Schedule M)
  • Qualified Person (RP) required
  • Site inspection by FDA required
  • Renewal every 5 years
🏪

Sale / Retail Licence

Required to sell drugs to the public. Form 20 (retail) or Form 21 (wholesale). Issued by District FDA office.

  • Registered Pharmacist required (retail)
  • Separate licence for Schedule H/H1/X drugs
  • Proper storage conditions required
  • Prescription drugs need valid Rx
  • Renewal every 5 years
✈️

Import Licence

Required to import drugs into India. Form 10 for bulk drugs; Form 10A for formulations. Issued by CDSCO (Central).

  • Country of origin certificate required
  • GMP certificate of manufacturer
  • New drugs need additional CDSCO approval
  • CoA (Certificate of Analysis) required
  • Narcotics/psychotropics need additional NOC
🚢

Export Licence / NOC

Exports regulated under DGFT and Drugs & Cosmetics Act. No Objection Certificate (NOC) required for certain categories.

  • Free Sale Certificate issued by CDSCO
  • GMP certificate for destination country
  • Export of narcotics requires DEA/NCB NOC
  • WHO-GMP certification often required
  • Country-specific requirements apply
🏅

GMP Certification

Good Manufacturing Practice (Schedule M) is the minimum standard for pharmaceutical manufacturing in India.

  • Premises, equipment & personnel standards
  • Quality control lab requirements
  • Batch records and documentation
  • WHO-GMP for export markets
  • US FDA / EU EMA for regulated markets
📋

No Objection Certificate (NOC)

NOC is required in specific situations — new site, change of premises, narcotic/psychotropic drugs, and clinical trial imports.

  • Issued by State or Central authority
  • Mandatory for clinical trial drugs
  • Required for change of constitution/ownership
  • Narcotics NOC from NCB (Home Ministry)
  • Dual-use chemicals need DRDO clearance

Patent & Intellectual Property

Product · Process · Trademark · Data

India's IP framework is governed by the Patents Act, 1970 (amended 2005), the Trade Marks Act, 1999, and the Patents Rules 2003. The landmark 2005 amendment brought India in compliance with TRIPS agreement under WTO.

🔬

Product Patent

Protects the drug molecule itself — its chemical structure and composition. India allows product patents for pharmaceuticals since 2005 (post-TRIPS compliance).

  • Term: 20 years from filing date
  • Section 3(d) bars trivial modifications
  • Prevents generic entry during term
  • Can be licensed to generate royalties
  • Valid after grant + 20-year term
⚗️

Process Patent

Protects the method or process used to manufacture a drug. A generic company may make the same molecule using a different process without infringement.

  • Term: 20 years from filing date
  • India's historical strength (pre-2005)
  • Allows innovation in synthesis routes
  • Often used by API manufacturers
  • Can be reverse-engineered by competitors
™️

Trademarks

Protects brand names, logos, and trade dress of pharmaceutical products. Essential for brand recognition and preventing counterfeit drug confusion.

  • Term: 10 years (renewable indefinitely)
  • Registered under Trade Marks Act 1999
  • Register with CGPDTM (IP India)
  • INN (generic name) cannot be trademarked
  • ® mark only after registration
🔒

Data Exclusivity

Prevents regulatory authorities from using the originator's clinical trial data to approve generic versions for a defined exclusivity period, even after patent expiry.

  • Not yet enacted in India (proposed)
  • US: 5 years NCE, 12 years biologics
  • EU: 8+2+1 year data exclusivity
  • Protects investment in clinical trials
  • TRIPS-Plus obligation in FTA negotiations

Patent Filing Process in India

1
Prior Art SearchSearch Patent databases (Espacenet, USPTO, IP India) to confirm novelty of your invention
2
Provisional / Complete ApplicationFile provisional application to establish priority date; complete specification within 12 months
3
PublicationApplication published 18 months from filing; early publication can be requested
4
Request for Examination (RFE)File RFE within 48 months of priority date; examination begins after request
5
Response to Office Actions (FER)Respond to First Examination Report objections from Patent Examiner
6
Grant & PublicationPatent granted and published in Patent Journal; 20-year term begins from filing date

Section 3(d) — India's Unique Provision

Section 3(d) of the Patents Act prevents the grant of patents on new forms (salts, esters, polymorphs, isomers) of known drugs unless they show significantly enhanced efficacy. This controversial provision was upheld in the landmark Novartis v. Union of India (2013) case regarding Gleevec (imatinib), and is considered a vital safeguard for affordable generic medicines in India.

Prevents evergreening Protects generic access TRIPS-compliant Novartis v. India (2013)
